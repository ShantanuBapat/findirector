## Findings

**compute vs. lookup was clean, not confused.** The boundary we expected to be
hardest turned out perfect: compute is 21/21 with zero leakage to lookup, and
lookup has perfect recall (30/30). The likely reason is that the labeler's
compute/lookup disagreements were resolved during labeling (plus 3 surgical
overrides), so by training time that boundary was consistent — and a consistent
boundary is learned sharply.

**The errors cluster on judgment calls, not topic or format.** All 5
misclassifications involve deciding what the user "really" wants:

- **clarify is the weak spot (recall 0.842).** Of 19 true clarify cases, 3 were
  answered instead of clarified — 1 as lookup, 2 as research. The model errs
  toward *acting* over *asking a follow-up* on ambiguous queries.
- **decline → lookup (2 cases).** Two queries that should have been declined were
  treated as lookups. This is the most product-relevant error: for a financial
  assistant, the model occasionally oversteps a compliance guardrail rather than
  refusing.
- **lookup is the "attractor" class.** Its only weakness is precision (0.909):
  when unsure, the model defaults to "just look it up." This single tendency
  explains most of the off-diagonal structure.

## Next steps

- **Targeted data, not blanket data.** Marginal training examples should focus on
  the two weak boundaries: ambiguous-but-answerable queries labeled `clarify`,
  and `decline` edge cases near the lookup boundary. Adding examples elsewhere
  would waste effort — the rest of the taxonomy is already near-perfect.
- **Inspect the 2 parse errors.** Low rate (1.3%), but worth confirming whether
  it's truncation (`max_new_tokens`) or genuine format drift.
- **Batching (optional).** ~5.7 s/example is unbatched generation. Left-padding
  is already configured, so batched generation would cut runtime substantially if
  this eval is re-run often.
